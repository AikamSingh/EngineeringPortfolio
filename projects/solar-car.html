Solar Car Steering Wheel

The steering wheel is the only thing a solar car driver touches. It has to show the right data, take reliable inputs, and never fail — across deserts, mountain passes, and 2,800+ miles of road.

Timeline Jul 2025 – Present
Tools Altium, STM32Cube IDE, C
Status 2026 deployed · 2027 in design

The Problem

Michigan Solar Car competes in multi-day events — the big one is the World Solar Challenge across the Australian outback. The steering wheel carries all the driver inputs and displays the telemetry the driver needs to make real-time decisions. If it fails, the car stops.

The 2025 wheel had a few known problems going into Solar Trek 2026. Push-to-talk had persistent noise that made radio communication frustrating. The OLED was hard to read in direct sunlight. And the PCB didn't fit cleanly into the enclosure — assembly was a pain, and the mechanical team had to work around it. None of these were catastrophic, but they added up. The team wanted a cleaner design before the next long trip.

Requirements

Before touching Altium, I mapped out the hard and soft requirements for the system:

  • CAN bus communicationRequired — all vehicle data
  • DisplayOLED graphical (2026), targeting LCD (2027)
  • Push-to-talkLow-noise hardware implementation
  • Torque / regen paddlesAnalog input to motor controller
  • Panel-mounted hardwareDust ingress reduction
  • Power sourceVehicle battery energy storage
  • EnvironmentalSurvive Australian outback conditions
  • Always-visible telemetrySpeed, signals, hazard, battery

Design Process

I started with the schematic in Altium, using the 2025 design as a reference for what to keep and what to change. The MCU choice was easy — the existing board used an STM32, so I stayed with that family. Switching would have meant rewriting the entire firmware stack for no real benefit.

For the CAN bus transceiver, I chose one compatible with what the rest of the vehicle already used. That mattered for testing — I could validate CAN communication with the motor controller early, without worrying about signal-level mismatches or termination differences.

The steering wheel is powered off the vehicle battery, so I added filtering and protection on the input rail. The battery voltage can spike when large loads switch on the vehicle's power distribution board, and the last thing I wanted was the steering wheel browning out mid-race.

For layout, I set up a solid ground plane early. The board mixes analog and digital signals — PTT audio, button inputs, and CAN bus — so I kept the analog traces short and separated from the switching signals. It's not a high-frequency board, but sloppy routing had caused PTT issues before.

I coordinated with the mechanical team in Siemens NX before finalizing the layout. One of the 2025 board's biggest problems was that connector and button positions didn't line up with the enclosure cutouts. This time I got the exact hole locations and mounting points from the CAD model and matched the layout to them before sending to fab.

All the buttons are panel-mounted — threaded hardware that screws directly into the enclosure, which keeps mechanical stress off the PCB pads. The OLED connects over SPI. The CAN transceiver runs to a dedicated connector that mates with the vehicle's harness.

Firmware

The firmware runs on FreeRTOS. I needed two reliable loops running independently — one for the display update, one for CAN receive — and a bare-metal superloop wasn't going to cut it. If the display took too long, the CAN receive handler would slip. With RTOS I could give the CAN task higher priority and let it preempt the display refresh if a message came in.

Decision: I rewrote the firmware from scratch instead of patching the 2025 code. The old firmware had accumulated workarounds that made the task timing hard to reason about. Starting clean with clearly scoped RTOS tasks took roughly the same amount of time and was far easier to debug during bring-up.

The CAN bus carries all the vehicle state. The steering wheel receives speed, battery level, and turn signal state from the rest of the car. It sends back driver inputs — torque request, regen braking level, push-to-talk state, and hazard toggle. Message IDs are defined in the team's shared DBC file so every node on the bus agrees on the signal format.

Challenges

PTT Noise

The push-to-talk issue was a ground loop. The PTT audio path shared a ground return with the CAN transceiver, and switching activity on the CAN side was coupling noise into the audio line. I traced it with an oscilloscope — the noise spike was clearly correlated with CAN traffic. The fix was separating the ground returns and adding a small ferrite bead on the audio line. It wasn't obvious until I saw the scope trace side-by-side with the CAN bus activity.

Mechanical Assembly Mismatch

The 2025 board's connector placement didn't match the enclosure cutouts — a few millimeters off on one axis. Assembly required filing the cutout slightly, which put sideways stress on the connector pads. For 2026 I coordinated with the mechanical team earlier and verified every panel-mount position against the CAD model before finalizing the layout. The fit was noticeably better, though not perfect. The lesson was that shared drawings aren't good enough — you need to check against the actual CAD.

Screen Readability in Direct Sunlight

OLEDs are emissive — they generate their own light. In direct sunlight, ambient light overwhelms the display. I tried cranking brightness to maximum and inverting the display in software (white background, black text), but neither helped enough. The 2027 redesign is switching to a character LCD. Reflective displays use ambient light instead of fighting it, which makes them actually easier to read outdoors. It's counterintuitive until you understand the physics.

Results

The 2026 steering wheel was deployed on Solar Trek — a 2,000+ mile cross-country drive from Michigan to California. I was part of the race crew, driving the car and maintaining the electrical systems on the road.

The board ran without steering wheel electrical failures during the trek. PTT noise was noticeably reduced — drivers stopped complaining about radio chatter. The telemetry layout got positive feedback from the other drivers, particularly the battery readout placement. A few things still need work for 2027, mainly the OLED readability in afternoon sun.

2027 Redesign

Based on Solar Trek experience, I'm leading the 2027 steering wheel redesign. The main goals:

  • DisplayOLED → 4-line LCD character display
  • Screen placementEvaluating on-wheel vs. column-mounted
  • Column detection sensorIMU or shorted-connection approach
  • Button hardwareTaller panel-mount buttons, new manufacturer
  • PCB integrationAltium into Siemens NX early in cycle
  • Regen paddlesEvaluating repositioning for ergonomics

Safety requirement: The column-detection sensor is race-critical — if the steering wheel is disconnected from the column, torque commands must be disabled to prevent the car from moving uncontrolled. The sensor needs to be reliable and overridable for diagnostics.

Tools & Validation

The hardware side was designed entirely in Altium Designer — schematic capture, PCB layout, and DRC. Mechanical coordination happened in Siemens NX, sharing reference geometry with the enclosure team. Firmware was written in C using FreeRTOS, developed in STM32CubeIDE.

Altium Designer STM32 C / FreeRTOS CAN bus OLED / SPI Siemens NX

Before the board left the bench I validated signal integrity on the CAN lines with an oscilloscope and confirmed message traffic with a logic analyzer. The real validation was Solar Trek itself — 2,800+ miles of driving is a better stress test than anything I could have set up in a lab.

Lessons Learned

The biggest thing I'd do differently: get the Altium layout into Siemens NX earlier. I thought shared dimension drawings were enough for mechanical coordination, but drawings don't catch everything. When the board arrived, two connector positions were off. Not a showstopper, but it cost time during assembly that a CAD check would have caught.

I also underestimated firmware bring-up time. The hardware came up fast — I had the OLED displaying data and CAN traffic flowing within a day. But the CAN message timing had edge cases that took a while to track down. A driver command could be lost if it arrived during a display refresh. Going forward I'd write the test harness and timing-stress tests before the board arrives, not after.

The OLED choice is the one I'd reconsider most. I knew reflective LCDs were better for outdoor readability — I just didn't push back hard enough on the decision before we committed. That's now a 2027 fix instead of a 2026 feature.
